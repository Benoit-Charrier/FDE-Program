
## Deliverable 2 - Delegation Analysis

### Fully Agentic

1. Intake and normalize inbound contracts from PDF or DOCX into machine-readable text.
Why: this is deterministic, repetitive, and low-judgment work.

2. Extract and label the target clause families from the playbook: liability, DPA, termination, IP ownership, SLA, governing law, and indemnity.
Why: the task is pattern matching against a fixed ontology rather than legal strategy.

3. Compare extracted clauses against approved playbook positions and produce a clause-by-clause variance report.
Why: the comparison is rules-based and auditable if the playbook is structured.

4. Prepare a review packet with source snippets, confidence scores, recommended routing, and draft redlines where policy allows.
Why: assembling evidence is administrative work that benefits from speed and consistency.

5. Track queue metrics, SLA timers, and workload dashboards.
Why: these are operational workflows, not legal judgments.

### Agent-Led With Human Oversight

1. Recommend whether a contract is standard, playbook-negotiable, or senior-lawyer escalation.
Why: this is the highest-value agent task, but misrouting has real legal consequences, so a human should remain accountable for accepting the route in early deployments.

2. Draft fallback redlines for deviations that are explicitly covered by the negotiation playbook.
Why: the agent can apply pre-approved language quickly, but a human needs to confirm that the factual context matches the allowed fallback.

3. Summarize risk and explain why a clause is outside playbook tolerance.
Why: summarization is efficient for the agent, but the legal team must verify that the explanation is not hiding nuance.

4. Pre-fill approval records for named lawyer sign-off on negotiated clauses.
Why: the agent can gather the packet, but the sign-off itself must remain human.

### Human-Led

1. Define the negotiation playbook, fallback language, and paralegal authority boundaries.
Why: these are policy decisions that reflect business risk appetite, not text-processing tasks.

2. Approve any outbound counteroffer that changes clause language.
Why: the general counsel has made this a hard control, and legal accountability cannot be delegated.

3. Resolve novel, ambiguous, low-confidence, or strategically sensitive clauses.
Why: these require legal reasoning, commercial tradeoff judgment, and sometimes relationship context the agent will not have.

4. Approve exceptions where business stakeholders want to accept risk outside the normal playbook.
Why: exception handling is organizational governance, not automation.
